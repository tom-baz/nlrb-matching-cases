## A publishable comparison design

For an academic article, I would not try to justify one method with a single global precision number. ER and clustering literatures both warn that evaluation metrics can rank methods differently, and name-disambiguation work often reports **multiple external metrics plus facet-level breakdowns** rather than one headline score. In your case, that means the paper should treat the comparison as a **multi-criterion evaluation** with separate lenses for trivial links, non-trivial links, blocker coverage, and downstream usefulness. That is more publishable than a winner-take-all framing, and it is more consistent with how modern ER and name-disambiguation systems are evaluated. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn22academia0Ņłéturn17academia1Ņłéturn18view0Ņłü

A defensible comparison section should evaluate at least the following:

| Evaluation target | What to report | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Accepted-pair precision | Blinded manual precision with Wilson intervals | Direct evidence on false positives |
| Non-trivial accepted-pair precision | Same, but excluding trivial identical-preprocessed-name links | Prevents exact-equality cases from dominating |
| Establishment-link yield | Unique R cases linked, unique C cases linked, pairs linked | Shows practical recall/value for the task |
| Conditional recall | Within a restricted plausible-candidate pool, not the full Cartesian product | Global recall is not estimable in a meaningful way |
| Blocking performance | Whether true pairs co-occur in the same candidate block | Separates blocker failure from matcher failure |
| Firm-clustering quality | External clustering metrics on a hand-labeled name benchmark | Necessary for firm-level aggregation claims |

That design is consistent with the fact that ER pipelines have separable **blocking** and **matching** stages, and that blocking criteria are routinely evaluated on their own in the literature. Blocking papers explicitly optimize pipelines around candidate-set coverage, often using measures such as pair completeness alongside efficiency criteria, because you cannot interpret downstream performance correctly if the blocker already excluded true matches. Your current sampling framework is a strong start, but for an article I would expand it in two ways. ## A firm-level clustering validation framework

If the long-run goal is firm-level aggregation, you need a validation strategy that is **independent of the RŌåöC establishment-matching task**. The best way to do that is to create a **name-only clustering benchmark** on a sampled subset of the name universe. Author name disambiguation is the closest methodological analogue: it is also a large-scale entity-resolution problem where the real task is to partition mentions into entity clusters. That literature commonly evaluates systems using external clustering metrics such as **pairwise F1**, **B-cubed F1**, and related split/lump diagnostics, rather than only pairwise match rates on one downstream task. Those metrics are also computationally feasible at very large scale. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn17academia1Ņłéturn18view0Ņłü

Concretely, I would sample a few hundred **ambiguous blocks or connected components** from the clustered company-name universe and manually partition every name in each sampled block into true firm identities. The stratification should be purposeful: very small blocks, medium blocks, near-cap blocks, merged blocks, abbreviation-heavy blocks, suspected OCR blocks, cross-source blocks, and blocks containing both RC and CA names. Once those blocks are hand-labeled, evaluate the clustering with at least pairwise F1 and B-cubed F1, and report performance by block size and textual difficulty. That gives you direct evidence about whether the clustering is usable for firm-level aggregation, independent of city and date restrictions. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn17academia1Ņłéturn18view0Ņłéturn33view0Ņłü

It is also important to evaluate the **blocker and the clusterer separately**. Your own failure analysis suggests that roughly two-thirds of the observed cluster misses came from the blocking layer or from names never entering the LLM comparison stage, not from the LLM making the wrong decision after seeing both names. ER pipeline research treats blocking and matching as distinct components for exactly this reason, and active-learning ER work explicitly warns that blocking can miss important regions of the product space and depress recall. For your project, that means you should compute blocker coverage over adjudicated true same-firm pairs: among hand-labeled positives, what fraction were ever placed in the same block or candidate set? That number may be more informative than more prompt tuning. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn0file3Ņłü ŅłĆciteŅłéturn35academia0Ņłéturn35academia2Ņłü

A second independent validation layer should be **stability**. LLM-CER itself emphasizes that clustering quality depends on set size, diversity, record variation, and ordering. Cluster-validation research likewise recommends stability-based validation, especially when full ground truth is unavailable. In your case, rerun a sampled subset of blocks several times while varying record order, representative selection, or even model version, then compute a co-clustering matrix: for each pair of names, how often do they end up in the same cluster? That frequency becomes a much more useful ŌĆ£confidenceŌĆØ measure than a one-off label from the LLM. It also directly answers your concern that the clustering currently lacks an agreement score. I would treat **internal validity indices** such as silhouette or related embedding-space cohesion measures only as secondary diagnostics. Recent work on deep clustering evaluation argues that internal metrics can be misleading when applied to raw high-dimensional data or to learned embedding spaces, because the geometry itself may distort the assessment. So if you compute internal indices over name embeddings, use them for debugging, not as headline evidence in the paper. The publishable evidence should come from external labels, validation splits, and stability. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn29view0Ņłü

## How to recover confidence and partial recall evidence

You are right that recall is the hardest part. In an open-world linkage problem with hundreds of thousands of names and a vanishingly small positive rate, **global recall over the full candidate universe is neither practical nor especially meaningful**. Your own evaluation guide says so, and broader ER benchmark work supports that caution. A much better target is **conditional recall within a plausible candidate pool**. In your setting, that pool could be defined as same state, same or fuzzy-matching city, overlapping date window, and either moderate string similarity or shared blocking/candidate-set membership. That makes the denominator both reviewable and substantively relevant to the establishment-linkage task. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn0file5Ņłü ŅłĆciteŅłéturn16view0Ņłü

To estimate that conditional recall, create a new **neither-method audit**. Draw a stratified sample from plausible pairs that neither pipeline linked, but do not sample uniformly. Oversample regions where misses are most likely: near-threshold fuzzy pairs, top embedding neighbors that landed in different blocks, fallback/non-clustered names, and names with strong lexical overlap but no link. Because that sampling is deliberately biased, analyze it with weights or performance estimators that account for sampling design. PatentsViewŌĆÖs ER evaluation paper is especially relevant here: it proposes data collection and tailored estimators that explicitly account for sampling biases in order to compare disambiguation algorithms more credibly. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn15view0Ņłü

For the blocker specifically, use a **pair-completeness style audit**. Blocking research routinely evaluates whether true matches were retained in the candidate set before matching. In your case, take all adjudicated true same-firm pairs from the hand-labeled benchmark and ask: did the two names appear in at least one common block or candidate neighborhood? That directly estimates the blockerŌĆÖs recall ceiling. If that figure is low, no amount of better prompting inside the LLM step will fix the main problem. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn25view0Ņłéturn35academia0Ņłü

For confidence, I would avoid relying on the LLMŌĆÖs own verbal certainty. Recent calibration studies show that LLM confidence expression is a real challenge and can remain misaligned with correctness. A better operational confidence measure is **consensus**: agreement across reruns, agreement between a clustering decision and a secondary verifier, or agreement between clustering and high-threshold fuzzy evidence. If you want a single score, define it from observable pipeline behavior, not from the modelŌĆÖs self-description. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn11academia1Ņłéturn11academia3Ņłü

If you ultimately want uncertainty-aware downstream firm-level estimates, a more ambitious option is to add a **probabilistic post-scoring layer**. Bayesian and probabilistic record-linkage work shows that uncertainty can be carried forward through posterior distributions or approximate probabilities, instead of being collapsed into one hard link set. You do not need a full Bayesian overhaul immediately, but the principle is useful: whenever possible, keep enough information so you can run downstream analyses on both a conservative core and an expanded hybrid set.
